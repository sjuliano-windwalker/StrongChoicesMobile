What about drugs like Spice and bath salts?

Both Spice and bath salts were recently moved to the DEA's list of Schedule 1 illegal substances—so they actually are illegal now. They are also banned in the Army. Originally, Spice was popular because there was no drug test for it--but now there are tests for it. Regardless, it doesn't matter if a drug is legal or not--problems with drugs come from the impairment they produce. These problems are obvious things like car crashes, fights, and relationship or legal problems, but also more subtle things like muddy thinking, depression, and anxiety. The only low-risk choice for these substances is to not use them at all.

If doctors prescribe drugs, doesn't that make them safe?

These drugs require a prescription because they come with a level of risk. Doctors and pharmacists are trained to manage that risk, and using prescription drugs in a way not prescribed by a doctor is high-risk. Also, non-prescribed use of prescription drugs is often--but not always--done to produce impairment. Remember that impairment is always high-risk, because it puts us in line for problems.



All this is also true of using over-the-counter drugs to get high. There are specific uses indicated in the packaging and warnings about misuse, and using an over-the counter-substance also bypasses the safety checks that allow the substance to be sold without a prescription. There are both impairment and health risks linked to such use.

What is a standard drink?

One standard drink is 0.6 ounces of pure (100%) alcohol.



Most (but not all!!) liquor is 80-proof (or 40%) alcohol—which means that a shot, which is about 1.5 ounces, is equal to one standard drink.



A 12-ounce beer, as long as it is 5% alcohol, is also equal to one standard drink—but some beer brands contain more than 5%.



A 5-ounce glass of wine that is 12% alcohol is one standard drink as well.

I've heard marijuana is safer than alcohol, and you can't get addicted to it. Is that true?

Comparing substances to one another is not helpful if we are interested in reducing our risk as much as possible. Any substance that produces impairment can result in impairment problems, and substances that produce impairment are likely to cause health problems as well.



There is actually a significant amount of research showing that marijuana does cause brain changes leading to addiction. The only research-based low-risk choice for drugs is abstinence.
